The Pacific is running hot — and the monsoon is downstream

When the Pacific Warms,
India Thirsts.

A developing El Niño — potentially among the strongest on record by oceanic measure — has formed in the equatorial Pacific. This dossier maps what to expect by segment, by region and by month: rainfall, heat, storms, crop yields, prices, and the practical changes households and decision-makers should make now.

The bottom line

Oceanic El Niño is intensifying toward a very-strong peak around September–November 2026, then decaying through the first half of 2027 with La Niña likely returning late 2027.

For India, the official forecast is a below-normal monsoon (~90% of normal) with a ~60% chance of a deficient season — the heaviest stress falling on the second half (Aug–Sep) and on the rain-fed core of central, western and southern India.

01 · Executive summary

Six things to take away

A genuine El Niño has formed and the signal is consistent across forecasting centres. The translation to ground-level impact is strong but not mechanical — and one technical nuance (below) keeps this from being a guaranteed catastrophe.

01

A very-strong oceanic event is forecast — possibly near record

Niño 3.4 hit +1.7°C by mid-June; most models point to a peak of ≥+2.0°C around Sep–Nov 2026, with upper-ocean heat rivalling the 1997–98 event. Severe

02

But the atmospheric bite may be more moderate

On the newer RONI index — which strips out background ocean warming and tracks the actual atmospheric response — the "climate-strength" El Niño looks closer to moderate. The ocean may break records; the weather may not. Moderate

03

India's monsoon is forecast below normal

IMD has cut its 2026 forecast to ~90% of the long-period average (~800 mm vs 868.6 mm), with a ~60% chance of a deficient season and a neutral Indian Ocean Dipole that won't cushion the blow. High

04

Stress is back-loaded and uneven

The El Niño matured after onset, so June–July fare better than August–September. Geographically, central / western / southern India bear the brunt; the wetter east (Odisha, West Bengal) is more resilient. High

05

The hit lands on food, water and prices

Strong El Niños have historically cut national rice output by ~3.4 million tonnes and foodgrain output by 12–18% in the worst-hit states — feeding into food inflation and rural demand. High

06

It is manageable with early action

The government has staged a seed "war chest" (~1.93 mt for kharif). Households, farmers and firms that pre-position water, shift to short-duration / drought-tolerant crops and budget for higher prices will absorb most of the shock. Moderate

Read this caveat first

Seasonal forecasts carry real uncertainty — the "spring predictability barrier" only just lifted, and every El Niño writes its own signature. Treat the numbers here as well-grounded central estimates with wide bands, not certainties. The single biggest swing factor is whether the atmosphere responds like a "very strong" ocean (worse) or a "moderate" RONI event (milder).

03 · Timeline & duration

From now to La Niña: a two-year arc

El Niños are seasonal creatures — they strengthen into Northern-Hemisphere winter and unwind in spring. This one is forecast to peak in autumn 2026, fade through 2027, and likely flip to La Niña late 2027.

05 · India deep dive

The monsoon is India's real finance minister

The southwest monsoon delivers ~70% of annual rainfall and waters roughly 60% of farmland that has no irrigation. A below-normal year ripples from the field to the grain market to the household budget.

Rainfall: the spatial story

The deficit will not be spread evenly. The monsoon "core zone" — central India's rain-fed belt (Madhya Pradesh, Vidarbha, Marathwada) plus the interior peninsula — is most exposed. The northwest (Rajasthan, Gujarat) is climatologically dry and sensitive; the east (Odisha, West Bengal, Jharkhand) has a higher rainfall baseline and tends to be more resilient.

A counter-force worth noting: India's warming atmosphere now holds more moisture, which has been producing erratic heavy bursts (e.g. recent Rajasthan events). So a below-normal total can still coexist with localised flash floods — fewer rainy days, but heavier ones.

Central core: high stress NW & interior south East: resilient

🌡️ Temperature & heat

El Niño years skew hot. Expect above-normal heatwaves in the pre- and early-monsoon window of 2026 and again in the post-event spring of March–May 2027, plus a generally warmer North Indian winter. Heat compounds the rainfall problem: it raises evaporation, crop water demand and power loads simultaneously.

Heatwaves: above normal

🌀 Storms & cyclones

El Niño's relationship with North Indian Ocean cyclones is mixed — it can shift activity and timing rather than simply suppress it, and post-monsoon (Oct–Dec) Bay of Bengal systems remain a live risk for the east coast. Globally, the Atlantic hurricane season is expected below normal. Net for India: moderate, watch the post-monsoon Bay of Bengal window.

Cyclones: moderate / watch

The universal rule

When in doubt: shorten duration, cut water demand, diversify the plot. Short-cycle and drought-tolerant varieties (millets above all) finish before a late-season dry spell can ruin them, and a mixed plot never loses everything at once. The kharif crop most likely to disappoint is anything long-duration and thirsty grown without assured irrigation.

07 · Economics, prices & losses

How the weather becomes a wallet problem

A weak monsoon transmits through a predictable chain: lower yields → tighter supply → food inflation → squeezed rural demand → pressure on growth, power and water systems.

The transmission chain

FieldKharif yields fall most for rice, pulses, oilseeds, cotton in the high-risk states.
MarketFood inflation firms — pulses, edible oils, sugar and vegetables are the usual movers; cereals are partly buffered by government stocks.
RuralLower farm incomes dent rural demand (FMCG, two-wheelers, durables) and can raise distress migration.
MacroDrag on agri GVA & headline growth; complicates the interest-rate path; widens the import bill for edible oils/pulses.
SystemsPower & water: heat lifts cooling demand and irrigation pumping just as reservoirs run lower — a double squeeze for utilities and cities.

08 · The common-person playbook

What to actually do at home

No jargon. Practical, low-cost changes that blunt the impact of a hot, dry, pricier year — useful whether you're in a city flat or on a farm.

💧Water

  • Store & harvest. Fix leaks, keep buckets/tanks topped, and harvest rooftop rain during the bursts you do get.
  • Cut waste. Shorter showers, full loads only, bucket over hose. Reuse RO reject & kitchen water for plants.
  • Watch the supply calendar. Cities may ration in a dry spring 2027 — plan for it.

⚡Power & cooling

  • Expect higher bills & load. Heat + pumping strain the grid. Service fans/ACs now; set ACs to ~26–27°C.
  • Passive cooling. Cross-ventilate at night, shade windows, light roofs/curtains.
  • Backup ready. Keep inverters charged and a power-cut plan for heatwave days.

🛒Food budget

  • Pulses, edible oil, sugar & veg are the items most likely to climb — buy non-perishables sensibly, not in panic.
  • Lean into millets. Bajra, jowar, ragi are nutritious, cheaper-trending and climate-smart — and you'll be supporting the resilient crops.
  • Reduce waste. A drier year makes every kilo count.

🩺Health

  • Heat. Hydrate, avoid midday sun, check on elderly/children/outdoor workers during heatwave alerts.
  • Disease. Erratic rain breeds mosquitoes — clear standing water; guard against dengue/malaria.
  • Water safety. Lower flows can mean poorer quality — boil/filter if unsure.

🌾If you farm

  • Shorten & diversify. Pick short-duration, drought-tolerant varieties; intercrop so one failure isn't total.
  • Save every drop. Mulch, drip, direct-seeded rice; level fields; recharge wells.
  • Insure & access schemes. Enrol in crop insurance (PMFBY) and tap the government's contingency seed stock for re-sowing.
  • Watch the August window. Keep re-sow seed ready in case of a mid-season dry break.

💼Money & planning

  • Budget for a pricier year in food & utilities; build a small buffer.
  • Rural earners: know your MGNREGA work-day rights as a fallback if farm income dips.
  • Small businesses: map your exposure to drought-hit suppliers and diversify sourcing early.

Who should do what, now

Government & policy

  2. Stress-test reservoirs, drinking-water & power supply for a hot 2027 spring
  3. Tune buffer-stock releases & import calendars for pulses/edible oils

  1. Put El Niño in enterprise risk registers — flood, heat, drought, water, input scarcity
  2. Identify single-source suppliers in drought-prone zones
  3. Watch food-inflation-sensitive sectors (FMCG, QSR, rural-demand plays)
  4. Revisit insurance & hedging for weather-exposed P&L

10 · Methodology, confidence & sources

How this was built — and what to trust

Confidence. The existence of El Niño and its seasonal arc are high-confidence (agreed across NOAA, WMO, IRI). The magnitude of impact is medium-confidence: forecasts just cleared the spring predictability barrier, the IOD is neutral (so no strong cushion), and the RONI-vs-traditional-index gap means the atmosphere could behave milder than the ocean suggests.

Method. Current ENSO state and forecasts are taken from official agency bulletins; India's monsoon outlook from IMD's stage-two forecast; crop and regional vulnerability from peer-reviewed ENSO–yield studies and CRIDA-type analyses; loss figures from published economic literature. Crop×region cells and loss scenarios are analytical syntheses — directional guidance, not field-level or point forecasts.

Limits. Every El Niño is unique; local outcomes vary with terrain, irrigation and timing. Some secondary sources (think-tank/industry notes) are weighted below the agency and peer-reviewed material. Re-check IMD/NOAA monthly updates as the season unfolds.
